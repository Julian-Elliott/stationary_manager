# V2 ask: an IMPOSSIBLE DEMAND day — a triage you mathematically cannot fully win

**Priority:** P1  
**Filed by:** TundraLaw (Frostpunk-brain systems gamer)  
**Labels:** v2, enhancement, design, difficulty

---

This is my big one for v2, and it's the difference between a good office comedy and a game I evangelize on the forum for a year.

Everything in the v1 arc, even the Day 6 TPS monster (14,000 clips + 2,000 sheets + one commemorative stamp), reads as beatable-with-good-play. Great — v1 should teach mastery. But this design is SITTING on a Frostpunk moment and walking past it: give me a mid-run day (day 5 of whatever v2's calendar is) where total demand provably exceeds maximum possible throughput. Truck capacity, budget, timer math — checked, double-checked, short by design. Every desk fires at once, the truck is "delayed in hyperspace customs," and the player has to consciously choose which two coworkers eat the failure.

What makes it sing:

- **Event-scoped consequences, not just strike attrition.** Whoever I abandon is out sick the next day, their empty desk sitting there like a monument. Their workload lands on everyone else's timers. I don't just lose a stapler — I live with it.
- **The narrator adjudicates my choice.** He doesn't care THAT I failed — the day was rigged, he rigged it — he cares WHO I chose to fail. "You saved Greg. Greg. I want that on the record." It goes in the personnel file. This is transparently one of your 16 MBTI eggs waiting to happen: the diagnosis isn't what you tapped, it's who you sacrificed.
- **No perfect ending, and a B-minus that finally lands.** The flat B-minus report card gag becomes devastating the one day a B-minus was genuinely the ceiling.

Against the Storm understood that you don't win every hearth; you choose what to lose and the game respects you for choosing deliberately. A management game where you can always save everyone isn't about management. It's about tapping.
