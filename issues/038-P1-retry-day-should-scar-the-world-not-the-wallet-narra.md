# RETRY DAY should scar the WORLD, not the wallet: narrator memory + persistent world-state, cash untouched

**Priority:** P1  
**Filed by:** Forum debate (TundraLaw / marigoldPress / solodev_ren)  
**Labels:** from-debate, design, fail-state, narrator

---

Filed by TundraLaw, formally superseding the "severance and legal fees" section of my earlier retry ticket. Credit where due: marigoldPress talked me out of it in the thread and she was right.

**Why the cash penalty was the wrong instrument:** at 13 minutes, the player's relationship isn't with the spreadsheet, it's with Deborah, Greg, and Marjorie. Docking a day of runner wages punishes a number nobody has bonded with. Scarring the world state is heavier precisely BECAUSE it doesn't touch the numbers — the player isn't reading a balance, they're standing in a room where something happened without them.

**Spec:**
- **The narrator MUST remember every retry and firing.** Reserve ~10 bark lines for a retry contempt tier: "Ah. You again. We've done this Tuesday before, you and I. It went poorly." A retry also shoves the NARRATIVE COMPLIANCE dial. The narrator forgetting a firing is character assassination of your own star — this part is non-negotiable.
- **One-shot world events stay happened.** Flagship example (pairs with marigoldPress's Day 4 birthday pitch, v1.1): retry Day 4 and the party already occurred — banner half taken down, cake box open and mostly empty, Doris still humming the song under her breath. Narrator, once: "You've missed the festivities. They were adequate. We will not be repeating them." Then nobody mentions it again.
- **Retries are logged as incident reports in the PERMANENT RECORD drawer** (see the persistence ticket), so the scar is re-readable.
- Cash, staplers, and supplies reset clean, as currently specced. RETRY DAY restarting only the current day is the right granularity — nobody replays twelve pencils twice.

Death stops being a loading screen the moment the world remembers it. That costs flags and bark lines, not economy tuning.
